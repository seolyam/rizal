## Context

In 1889, twenty young women from Malolos petitioned the Spanish colonial government to establish a night school so they could study Spanish and secular subjects. Rizal’s congratulatory letter praises their resolve, linking women’s education to the moral and civic regeneration of the nation.[14][28]

## Core Messages

- **Education as Empowerment.** Rizal argues that literate women raise principled citizens capable of scrutinizing injustice.[28]
- **Faith and Reason.** He calls for spirituality grounded in understanding rather than blind obedience, critiquing superstition imposed by friars.[20]
- **Domestic Sphere as Civic Seedbed.** Households guided by intelligent, courageous mothers become workshops for patriotism.[28]
- **Courage and Self-Respect.** The Malolos petition exemplifies how dignity and persistence challenge colonial paternalism.[14]

<Cite>
  “Let us be reasonable and open our children’s eyes so that they may guard their rights.”
</Cite>

## Structure of the Letter

1. **Commendation.** Celebrates the petition and frames it as a national victory.
2. **Guidance.** Offers counsel on education, faith, and morality.
3. **Exhortation.** Urges continued solidarity and vigilance against oppression.

## Historical Impact

The letter circulated clandestinely among reformists and inspired subsequent campaigns for women’s schooling. It reveals Rizal’s vision of women as intellectual partners in the freedom struggle—not passive symbols.[20]
